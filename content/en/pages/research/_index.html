The CIDS is committed to advancing research in the field of international dispute resolution, serving as a hub for scholarly inquiry and innovation. Its mission is to produce cutting-edge research that addresses contemporary challenges in arbitration and dispute settlement, contributing to both academic discourse and practical applications. By fostering interdisciplinary collaborations and engaging with leading experts, CIDS aims to generate impactful insights and develop new methodologies that enhance the effectiveness and fairness of international dispute resolution. The Center's research initiatives also strive to influence policy-making and improve the global legal framework governing international disputes.

Research in law, and in particular criteria for excellence in research, have over the last decades globally shifted from a service towards legal practice to a purpose in itself, seeking the advancement of knowledge and understanding. CIDS in part follows this trend but, aware of the importance of continued critical contributions by academia to the defence and development of pacific methods of dispute settlement, it also significantly engages in service to practice.

CIDS Digressions (outdated?)


The "CIDS Digressions" aim to foster innovative thinking, interdisciplinary research, and critical discussion of topical issues. They will enable researchers from various backgrounds to share ideas in a concise and efficient way within an unfettered intellectual framework. Contributions to the series, of a maximum of 1000 words in length, will be published online following a short and straightforward editorial process.
The CIDS Digressions in particular offer:

• A critical analysis of topical and engaging issues in international dispute settlement broadly speaking.

• An overview of current research in other academic fields offering contrasting, disruptive, or simply revealing insights into the resolution of international disputes.

• The gist of new, audacious, or simply relevant ideas, which deserve being shared and discussed in a straightforward, unconstrained or clear-cut way, either before or in parallel to a traditional peer-reviewed publication.

Contributions can be sent to clement.bachmann@unige.ch
